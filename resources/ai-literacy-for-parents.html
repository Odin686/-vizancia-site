For parents

AI literacy starts with a conversation.

Children do not need to become programmers to use AI well. They need practice asking good questions, protecting private information, checking claims, and knowing when a human should decide.

8 minute read•Reviewed July 14, 2026

Why now? In a February 2026 survey of U.S. teens ages 13–17, Pew Research Center found that 57% had used chatbots to search for information and 54% had used them for schoolwork. Ten percent said they do all or most of their schoolwork with chatbot help. Those figures describe a survey population, not every child, but they show why guidance is more useful than panic. Read Pew’s methodology and findings.

What AI literacy means

AI literacy is the ability to understand what an AI system can and cannot do, use it for an appropriate purpose, evaluate its output, and make responsible choices about people and data. UNESCO’s student framework groups these capabilities into a progression of understand, apply, and create across a human-centred, ethics, technique, and design dimension. See the UNESCO framework.

Five conversations to try this week

  1. “What do you think the system knows?” Separate a model’s confident wording from evidence that it is correct.
  2. “What should never go in a chat?” Practise leaving out full names, addresses, passwords, health details, private images, and another person’s information.
  3. “How could we check this?” Ask for a source, compare two reliable sources, and look for the date and context.
  4. “What part is yours?” For schoolwork, agree whether AI may brainstorm, explain, or give feedback—and what the learner must write and cite themselves.
  5. “When should we stop?” Make it normal to close the tool when it produces unsafe, upsetting, biased, or private content and tell a trusted adult.

A simple family routine

Use a three-step rhythm: Pause → Probe → Prove. Pause before sharing data or accepting an answer. Probe with a follow-up question such as “What are you assuming?” Prove important claims with a teacher, primary source, book, or experiment. This turns every AI interaction into a small lesson in judgment.

Talk about schoolwork without shame

Children may use chatbots because a task feels confusing, boring, or high-stakes. Start by asking them to show the process, not by assuming intent. Set a visible family rule: the learner remains the author, follows the teacher’s policy, and can explain every submitted idea in their own words. If AI use is not allowed, help them find another support such as a teacher, library, or peer.

What to model as an adult

  • Say “I’m not sure; let’s verify” when an answer sounds plausible but unsupported.
  • Demonstrate editing an AI draft instead of copying it.
  • Explain that a friendly tone is a design choice, not proof that a system understands feelings.
  • Review privacy settings together and choose tools with clear data practices.
